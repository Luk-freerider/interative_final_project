Beyond the Shutdown: Chicago's Adaptive Athletes Face Hidden Crisis

By Kevin Lu

Published: November 2025

The federal government shutdown ended without closing Chicago's adaptive sports programs—but it exposed a deeper crisis affecting the athletes who depend on them.

When the Chicago Skyhawks rolled onto the court at Jesse White Community Center for Wednesday night practice, everything looked normal. The wheelchair basketball team practiced their plays, took their shots, and finished another session. The government shutdown that had paralyzed federal agencies for weeks hadn't touched their programming.

But behind the scenes, some athletes were struggling in ways most people never saw.

"Some of our guys rely on SNAP to eat and survive. When that gets threatened, it affects people. We had players asking quietly for help."
— Pierre Nelson, Adaptive Sports Coordinator

Pierre Nelson, adaptive sports coordinator at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab and a coach within Chicago's wheelchair basketball community, said the shutdown revealed a critical gap: while adaptive sports programs operate almost entirely on private funding, the athletes themselves often depend on federal benefits just to survive.

Timeline: How the Shutdown Unfolded for Adaptive Athletes

Week 1
Shutdown Begins — Programs Continue
Federal agencies close, but Chicago's adaptive sports programs continue without interruption. Organizations like Shirley Ryan AbilityLab, Special Olympics Illinois, and Dare2tri rely on private funding and face no immediate operational impacts.
Week 2
Athletes Begin Feeling the Strain
Players who depend on SNAP benefits and Medicaid start worrying about disruptions. Some athletes quietly approach coaches and coordinators asking for help with food and basic needs.
Week 3
Emergency Support Mobilized
Coaches begin distributing donated gift cards and food to athletes in need. The assistance is provided discreetly—"not everybody needed it, but some did," Nelson recalls.
Resolution
Shutdown Ends, Questions Remain
President Trump signs bipartisan funding deal (Senate vote: 60-40). Federal agencies reopen, but disability advocates and athletes continue to worry about the next disruption and long-term threats to safety net programs.

"We were able to keep everything running," Nelson said. "The lab is all privately funded. From the sports side, nothing stopped." But he quickly added: "Some of our guys rely on SNAP to eat and survive. When that gets threatened, it affects people. We had players asking quietly for help, and we were giving out donated gift cards and food."

Debbie Hrncir, a Skyhawks member, described the psychological toll even when practices continued.

"It's scary when you don't know if your benefits will be there. A lot of us are dealing with injuries or health issues. We depend on Medicaid, on food assistance. Sports help keep us going—but life outside the gym gets harder when the government shuts down."
— Debbie Hrncir, Chicago Skyhawks Athlete

Historical Context: Why Adaptive Sports Lack Federal Support

Before the Education for All Handicapped Children Act (EAHCA) of 1975—the precursor to today's IDEA—children with disabilities were routinely barred from public schools.

Sports programs grew in similarly uneven ways, relying on local hospitals, nonprofits, and volunteers to create spaces for athletes with disabilities. This legacy of private funding continues today.

Linda Mastandrea, a two-time Paralympian, disability rights attorney, and former Shirley Ryan AbilityLab athlete, said the shutdown exposed problems that have existed for decades.

"It's always been tough because so little comes from the government," she said. "When I made the 1992 Paralympic team, the U.S. Olympic Committee didn't fund us at all. We had to raise our own money just to compete for our country."

While progress has improved—some Paralympians now receive stipends or health insurance—community-level programs still face inconsistent support. More concerning, Mastandrea said, is how broader federal cuts could ripple far beyond sports.

"Many people with disabilities depend on Medicaid for health care or home services," she explained. "If those programs shrink, the impact is immediate. And if the Department of Education weakens its Special Education Division, students with disabilities lose protections. Those are direct, real consequences."

Both Mastandrea and Nelson emphasized how deeply sports intersect with health, mobility, mental well-being, and long-term opportunity for people with disabilities.

"Sports change lives. If the government invested consistently—even at the grassroots level—you'd see better health outcomes, more educational opportunities and greater employability for people with disabilities."
— Linda Mastandrea, Two-time Paralympian & Disability Rights Attorney

For Chicago's adaptive athletes, the shutdown ended without major damage to programming. But the concerns it exposed—about medical care, food security, and the near-absence of federal support for adaptive sports—remain, even as the gym lights stay on.

"Put politics aside and put people first," Nelson said.
